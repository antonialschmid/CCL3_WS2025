Gratitude Garden

Creative Coding Lab 3 – Winter Semester 2025

Concept

Gratitude Garden is a wellbeing-focused Android app that explores gratitude journaling in a calm and low-pressure way. The project was developed as part of Creative Coding Lab 3 and uses a single plant as a visual metaphor for emotional growth and reflection.

Many existing gratitude apps rely heavily on text, statistics, or streak-based systems, which can feel repetitive or stressful for users. Gratitude Garden takes a different approach by visualising progress through the gradual growth of one plant, without creating competition or performance pressure.

Each gratitude entry contributes to the plant’s development, moving through predefined growth stages over time. The focus of the app is not productivity, but mindful reflection and emotional awareness. The plant serves as a gentle companion that represents the user’s personal gratitude journey.

Idea & Motivation

Many people struggle to maintain a regular gratitude journaling habit. Existing solutions often feel rigid or competitive. Gratitude Garden was created to allow reflection without pressure, using simple visual evolution.

By visualising emotional progress as plant growth, the app supports emotional wellbeing rather than productivity tracking. This concept encourages users to reflect in a calm and aesthetic way.

Implementation

The app is built for the Android platform using Kotlin and follows the MVVM architecture pattern. Data is stored locally via Room (SQLite) to ensure privacy and usability without internet access.

The interface consists of a garden view with one central plant, a screen to add daily entries, and a calendar view showing past contributions.

Technical Overview

The project is implemented using Kotlin (Android) with an MVVM architecture, Room for local persistence, and a focus on offline-first usability.

Data such as entries and plant stage are stored locally, preserving privacy and enabling use without an internet connection.

User Test Plan

To evaluate the usability and clarity of the Gratitude Garden app, a structured user test plan was developed. The goal of the evaluation is to identify usability issues early, assess the comprehensibility of interactions, and validate design decisions.

1. Heuristic Evaluation

Before conducting user tests, a heuristic evaluation was performed on the initial prototype. The evaluation was based on common usability heuristics such as visibility of system status, consistency, error prevention, and user control, as discussed in UEE.

Strengths

  • Clear visual hierarchy and consistent styling across screens.
  • Navigation structure (Garden, Add Entry, History) is easy to understand.
  • Visual feedback (colors, highlights, calendar dots) supports orientation.

Identified Usability Issues

  • Some interactions (e.g. date selection via calendar or month dropdown) may not be immediately obvious.
  • Icons without text labels rely on prior user familiarity.
  • The calendar view may require explanation for first-time users.

These findings informed the design of the user test tasks and research questions.

2. Initial Hypotheses & Research Questions

  • H1: Users can navigate between Garden, History, and Add Entry without external help.
  • H2: Users understand how to add a new gratitude entry, including selecting a date and mood.
  • H3: Users can interpret the calendar and its visual indicators (dots, colors, highlights).

Additional exploratory questions include:

  • Q1: Do users understand the meaning of colored calendar days and rings?
  • Q2: At which points do users feel uncertain or confused?

3. Data to Collect

Both quantitative and qualitative data will be collected during the usability test.

Quantitative Data

  • Task completion rate (completed / not completed)
  • Time on task
  • Number of errors or incorrect interactions
  • Questionnaire results (e.g. SUS or short Likert-scale questions)

Qualitative Data

  • User comments during and after tasks
  • Observed hesitation or confusion
  • Open-ended feedback on design and interaction

4. Methods, Tasks & Materials

Method

  • Moderated usability test using the think-aloud method
  • Within-subject design (all participants perform the same tasks)
  • Small sample size suitable for early-stage design evaluation

Tasks

  • Navigate to the Garden screen and explain what is visible.
  • Add a new gratitude entry for today.
  • Change the date of an entry.
  • Navigate to History and find an entry from another month.
  • Open an existing entry and describe its content.

Materials

  • Interactive prototype / running app
  • SUS questionnaire
  • Observation notes taken by the evaluator

5. Timing & Iteration

The usability test may be conducted either early during the Creative Coding Lab to identify major usability issues, or later before the final presentation to validate the refined design.

6. Participants

The test will be conducted with at least five participants. Participants are young adults aged approximately 20–35 and regular smartphone users. None of the participants will have prior experience with the Gratitude Garden app.

7. Procedure

The usability test will be conducted as a moderated, in-person session lasting approximately 10–20 minutes.

  • Introduction and consent
  • Execution of predefined tasks
  • Completion of a short questionnaire (SUS)
  • Open feedback discussion

During the test, observations will focus on task success, hesitation, errors, and user comments.
